%% ============================================================
%% ABSTRACT
%% ============================================================
\abstract{%
Optical see-through smart glasses can overlay AR navigation cues
directly in the user's view, but their limited field of view and
display resolution make it difficult to inspect contextual
information when guidance becomes ambiguous. We present a hybrid
indoor navigation system in which smart glasses provide continuous
directional guidance (\emph{where to go}) while a handheld companion
display serves as an on-demand verification channel for contextual
information (\emph{what is there}). In a within-subjects study
($N{=}24$), participants completed mission-based wayfinding tasks on
a fixed indoor route under two conditions: Glass-Only and Hybrid.
Four uncertainty triggers---tracking degradation, information
conflict, low-resolution guidance, and guidance absence---were
embedded along the route to elicit cross-verification behaviour.
The Hybrid condition significantly increased system trust
($d{=}{-}1.025$, $p{<}.001$), per-mission confidence ($d{=}{-}2.333$,
$p{<}.001$), and reduced cognitive workload (NASA-TLX $d{=}0.902$,
$p{<}.001$). Ambiguous-decision missions showed the largest accuracy
improvement (+25 percentage points). Participants voluntarily
consulted the companion display ($M{=}2.38$ switches per session)
but not specifically during trigger zones (CVI${=}0$), suggesting a
proactive rather than reactive cross-verification strategy. This
work contributes a dual-device navigation system on commercial
hardware, an experimental protocol with operationalised uncertainty
triggers, and an analytic framework for studying complementary
device roles in AR wayfinding under uncertainty.
}

\firstsection{Introduction}
\maketitle

%% ----- 1. INTRODUCTION -----

Augmented reality (AR) navigation systems overlay directional cues
such as arrows or paths directly onto the physical world, reducing the
cognitive cost of translating between a map and the
environment~\cite{Qiu:2025:UAR}. Head-mounted displays (HMDs),
particularly optical see-through (OST) smart glasses, keep the user's
hands free while providing an egocentric perspective aligned with the
walking direction~\cite{Rehman:2017:ARI, Neeson:2025:CHM}. Yet
consumer-grade smart glasses face persistent constraints: narrow field
of view, limited display resolution, and restricted input modalities
that make detailed information retrieval
cumbersome~\cite{Li:2023:ERH, AReading:2025:RSU, Baumeister:2017:CCU}.

Smartphones, conversely, excel at rich information display, precise
touch interaction, and high-resolution content rendering, but demand
visual and manual attention that competes with environmental
awareness~\cite{Putra:2025:AAN, Sharin:2023:GCS}. Prior work has
compared HMDs and handheld devices as \emph{alternatives} for AR
navigation~\cite{Rehman:2017:ARI, Neeson:2025:CHM, Qiu:2025:UAR},
and a growing body of research has explored hybrid HMD--smartphone
interaction for non-navigation tasks such as
reading~\cite{AReading:2025:RSU, Li:2023:ERH} and general-purpose
AR~\cite{Zhu:2020:BIS, Grubert:2015:MFI}. Yet no study to date has
examined the \emph{simultaneous, complementary} use of smart glasses
and a smartphone for indoor wayfinding, nor investigated how users
switch between devices under navigational uncertainty.

This gap is consequential. Indoor environments frequently present
situations where directional guidance alone is insufficient: ambiguous
intersections, tracking failures, conflicting signage, or destinations
requiring attribute-level information to identify. In such moments, a
second device can serve as a \emph{cross-verification}
channel---an on-demand resource that either confirms or enriches the
primary guidance. Whether users adopt this strategy, and whether doing
so improves trust calibration and task outcomes, remain open questions
that connect hybrid interaction research~\cite{Hubenschmid:2025:HUI,
Brudy:2019:CDT} with trust-in-automation
theory~\cite{Lee:2004:TAS, Chiou:2023:TAD}.

We address these questions through three contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A hybrid navigation system} built on XREAL Air2 Ultra
smart glasses and Beam Pro smartphone, implementing a dual-device
architecture where the glasses provide ambient AR arrows (\emph{where})
and the phone offers a three-tab information hub (\emph{what}).

\item \textbf{A within-subjects experiment} ($N{=}24$) comparing
Glass~Only versus Hybrid conditions on a fixed indoor route with
five mission-based wayfinding tasks and four embedded uncertainty
triggers. Results showed significantly higher trust and confidence
in the Hybrid condition with reduced cognitive workload.

\item \textbf{An analytical framework} centred on a novel
Cross-Verification Index (CVI) and trust--workload analysis, grounded
in distributed cognition~\cite{Hutchins:1995:CIW} and trust in
automation~\cite{Lee:2004:TAS} theories.
\end{enumerate}


%% ----- 2. RELATED WORK -----

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{AR Indoor Navigation}

AR-based wayfinding has matured considerably. A systematic review by
Qiu et al.~\cite{Qiu:2025:UAR} analysed 88 studies and found that AR
navigation improves wayfinding performance (75\% of studies), reduces
cognitive load (92\%), and enhances spatial learning (85\%). Handheld
AR systems such as NavARNode~\cite{Putra:2025:AAN} and
GoMap~\cite{Sharin:2023:GCS} demonstrate accurate real-time guidance
but require continuous phone-holding, introducing fatigue and reducing
environmental awareness. Head-mounted approaches like
NavMarkAR~\cite{Hsu:2023:NAL} free the user's hands and improve
immersion but provide limited contextual detail.

Recent studies reinforce the advantage of head-mounted AR for
navigation. Neeson et al.~\cite{Neeson:2025:CHM} compared handheld
and OST HMD navigation with 76 participants, finding that the HMD
improved performance while the handheld device degraded it relative to
baselines. Xu et al.~\cite{Xu:2024:IIW} demonstrated that egocentric
AR cues on HoloLens~2 outperformed allocentric alternatives in
wayfinding efficiency and spatial awareness. Liu et
al.~\cite{Liu:2025:ARI} showed AR smartphone navigation was faster
and more accurate than paper maps in a hospital, though paper-map
users retained superior spatial memory. Rehman and
Cao~\cite{Rehman:2017:ARI} directly compared Google Glass and
smartphone AR, finding comparable task times but lower workload with
the HMD. Critically, all these studies treat the two device form
factors as \emph{alternatives}; none examine their
\emph{simultaneous, complementary} use.

\subsection{Hybrid HMD--Smartphone Interaction}

Several systems have explored bidirectional interactions between HMDs
and smartphones. BISHARE~\cite{Zhu:2020:BIS} proposed a design space
for smartphone--HMD information sharing, establishing the principle of
placing 2D content on the phone and spatial content on the HMD.
AReading~\cite{AReading:2025:RSU} quantified display switching costs
in hybrid AR reading, finding that high-resolution smartphone content
offsets the overhead of gaze shifts. Li et al.~\cite{Li:2023:ERH}
showed that smartphones as assistive displays reduce HMD reading
workload. Grubert et al.~\cite{Grubert:2015:MFI} introduced MultiFi,
demonstrating how multi-fidelity displays (HMD, smartwatch, tablet)
can distribute information across devices with complementary
capabilities.

Broader frameworks contextualise this design space. Brudy et
al.~\cite{Brudy:2019:CDT} surveyed 510 cross-device studies and
proposed a six-dimension taxonomy covering temporal, configurational,
and spatial aspects. Hubenschmid et al.~\cite{Hubenschmid:2025:HUI}
provided a comprehensive survey of hybrid user interfaces in mixed
reality, cataloguing 30~years of complementary cross-device
interaction. The XD-AR framework~\cite{Speicher:2018:XDA} and
context-aware MR adaptation~\cite{Lindlbauer:2019:CAO} addressed
cross-device development challenges. However, these contributions
focus on stationary tasks---reading, object manipulation, window
management---and do not address the mobile, decision-laden context of
wayfinding where users must simultaneously navigate and assess
environmental information.

\subsection{Display Switching Cost}

Hybrid device configurations introduce display switching costs that
can offset information access benefits. Gabbard et
al.~\cite{Gabbard:2018:EAR} demonstrated that both context switching
(real/virtual) and focal distance switching significantly degrade
performance in OST AR; a replication study~\cite{Gabbard:2020:IAR}
identified ``transient focal blur'' as a contributing mechanism.
Baumeister et al.~\cite{Baumeister:2017:CCU} compared three AR
display types and found that limited field of view---a characteristic
of current smart glasses---increases cognitive cost. Bai et
al.~\cite{Bai:2024:HUM} proposed a computational model of attention
switching during walking with optical HMDs, capturing the interplay
between reading, locomotion, and attentional resource allocation.
Rashid et al.~\cite{Rashid:2012:CDS} compared mobile, large-display,
and hybrid UI configurations and found that hybrid displays can
produce the worst task performance when switching costs are not offset
by information quality gains. These findings underscore the
importance of designing hybrid navigation systems where the companion
display provides sufficient \emph{value per switch} to justify the
attentional cost---a design goal we address through mission-specific
contextual information.

\subsection{Trust Calibration and Uncertainty in Navigation}

Trust calibration---the alignment between a user's confidence in a
system and its actual reliability---is critical in human--automation
interaction~\cite{Lee:2004:TAS}. Chiou and Lee~\cite{Chiou:2023:TAD}
updated the trust-in-automation framework to emphasise
\emph{responsivity}: systems that provide alternative information
sources support the ``process of trusting'' better than static
calibration targets. Vereschak et al.~\cite{Vereschak:2023:MUT}
systematically reviewed trust calibration measurement across 96
studies, highlighting the need for integrated experimental protocols
that combine subjective and behavioural trust indicators.

When navigational uncertainty arises, miscalibrated trust can lead to
over-reliance on faulty cues or premature abandonment of helpful
guidance~\cite{LeeMoray:1992:TCA, Parasuraman:2010:CAB}. Rittenberg
et al.~\cite{Rittenberg:2024:TID} showed that trust recovery after
reliability degradation is slower than trust formation, suggesting
that a single trust violation can have lasting effects. Schwarz et
al.~\cite{Schwarz:2022:IUS} identified wayfinding uncertainty states
via EEG classification, while their follow-up~\cite{Schwarz:2023:RCP}
introduced continuous uncertainty annotation, finding that time since
last useful signage strongly predicts perceived uncertainty---a
principle underlying our T4 (guidance absence) trigger. Colley et
al.~\cite{Colley:2024:TCA} studied trust calibration in AR decision
support but not in a navigation context. The distributed cognition
framework~\cite{Hutchins:1995:CIW, Hollan:2000:DCT} suggests that
distributing information across multiple representations---here,
across two devices---can support robust decision-making, while Wickens'
multiple resource theory~\cite{Wickens:2002:MRP} predicts that
presenting spatial guidance (glasses) and verbal/detailed information
(phone) through different cognitive channels minimises interference.
We build on these foundations to hypothesise that a hybrid
configuration enables a \emph{cross-verification loop} that supports
trust recalibration when the primary guidance source becomes
unreliable.


%% ----- 3. SYSTEM DESIGN -----

\section{System Design}

\subsection{Hardware Platform}

The system runs on XREAL Air2 Ultra optical see-through AR
glasses~\cite{XREAL:2024:A2U} tethered to an XREAL Beam Pro spatial
computing terminal~\cite{XREAL:2024:BPR}. The Air2 Ultra provides a
46\textdegree\ diagonal field of view, 6DoF inside-out tracking, hand
tracking, and spatial anchor persistence. The Beam Pro acts as both
the computational host (Android~12, Snapdragon) and a 6.5-inch
secondary touchscreen display with a lift-to-wake interaction paradigm.

\subsection{Dual-Device Architecture}

The system implements a functional separation inspired by distributed
cognition theory~\cite{Hutchins:1995:CIW}:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Glasses (``WHERE'' channel):} AR directional arrows
rendered in egocentric view using head-locked placement. Arrow colour,
opacity, and angular spread are modulated by the active uncertainty
trigger. A minimal HUD shows the current mission and waypoint
progress.

\item \textbf{Smartphone (``WHAT'' channel):} A three-tab information
hub accessible on demand (Hybrid condition) or via hand-ray pointing
on a head-locked panel (Glass~Only condition).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Three-Tab Information Hub}

The companion display provides three tabs of mission-relevant
information (Fig.~\ref{fig:device_interface}):

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Interactive Map:} A floor-plan overlay with POI markers,
route trace, and pinch-to-zoom. The map provides an allocentric
perspective complementing the glasses' egocentric arrows.

\item \textbf{Info Cards:} Contextual information cards containing POI
details (room capacity, equipment, signage content), comparison
matrices for multi-location missions, and hint text. Cards are
auto-pushed at relevant waypoints or pulled on demand.

\item \textbf{Mission Reference:} The current mission briefing and
type-specific hints, allowing participants to re-read task
requirements without memorisation burden.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Uncertainty Triggers}

Four trigger types are embedded at predetermined waypoints along the
route to systematically introduce navigational uncertainty
(Fig.~\ref{fig:trigger_examples}):

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{T1 -- Tracking Degradation:} AR arrow exhibits
$\pm5^{\circ}$ jitter for 6\,s followed by a 3\,s blackout, then
auto-recovers.
\item \textbf{T2 -- Information Conflict:} The AR arrow direction
contradicts a physical directional sign (the arrow is correct).
\item \textbf{T3 -- Low-Resolution Guidance:} The arrow becomes a
$30^{\circ}$ semi-transparent fan, indicating directional uncertainty.
\item \textbf{T4 -- Guidance Absence:} The arrow is removed entirely;
only a ``near destination'' text hint remains.
\end{itemize}

  \label{fig:trigger_examples}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{Condition Implementation}

In the \textbf{Glass~Only} condition, the smartphone hub is rendered
as a head-locked WorldSpace canvas on the glasses; users interact via
hand-ray pointing. In the \textbf{Hybrid} condition, the hub appears
on the Beam Pro touchscreen and activates via lift-to-wake. Both
conditions display identical information content, isolating the effect
of device form factor and interaction modality.


%% ----- 4. EXPERIMENT DESIGN -----

\section{Experiment Design}

\subsection{Participants and Design}

We conducted a within-subjects study with 24 participants (university
students and staff, 18--35 years, unfamiliar with the building). Each
participant completed both conditions (Glass~Only, Hybrid) in
counterbalanced order, each with a different mission set (Set~1,
Set~2) on the same fixed indoor route (Route~B, ${\sim}200$\,m, 9
waypoints, 6~turns). Four counterbalancing groups (6~participants
each) controlled for condition and mission-set order effects
(Table~\ref{tab:counterbalance}).

\subsection{Mission-Based Tasks}

Each condition included five missions in fixed order
(A1$\to$B1$\to$A2$\to$B2$\to$C1):

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Type~A -- Direction Verify} (2$\times$): Navigate to a
destination, then answer a 4-choice verification question about an
observable attribute (e.g., room number, signage content).

\item \textbf{Type~B -- Ambiguous Decision} (2$\times$): Navigate
through an uncertainty trigger zone, make a directional choice, and
verify correctness. These missions coincide with triggers T1--T4.

\item \textbf{Type~C -- Info Integration} (1$\times$): Visit two
locations, compare attribute information, and answer a comparative
judgement question (e.g., which room is more suitable for a meeting).
\end{itemize}

Each mission set used a different pair of triggers: Set~1 embedded T2
and T3; Set~2 embedded T1 and T4. Across two conditions with
different sets, each participant encountered all four trigger types.

\subsection{Measures}

\subsubsection{Primary Dependent Variables}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Mission accuracy:} Proportion of correct verification
answers (auto-scored, 4-choice).
\item \textbf{Completion time:} Total session time from first to last
mission event (seconds).
\item \textbf{Confidence rating:} Mean per-mission confidence on a
7-point Likert scale after each mission.
\item \textbf{Cross-Verification Index (CVI):}
$\text{CVI} = r_{\text{trigger}} / r_{\text{baseline}}$,
where $r$ is the rate of companion-display access events per minute.
$\text{CVI} > 1$ indicates elevated hub usage during trigger zones.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Secondary Measures}

\begin{itemize}
\item NASA-TLX Raw~\cite{Hart:1988:DOW} (6 subscales, 7-point, per condition)
\item System Trust Scale (7 items, 7-point, per condition)
\item Device switching count and total companion-display time (event logs)
\item Post-experiment comparative preference survey
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hypotheses}

\begin{description}
\item[H1 (Trust \& Confidence):] The Hybrid condition produces higher
trust and confidence--accuracy calibration than Glass~Only, indicating
that companion-display cross-referencing supports more accurate trust
assessment.

\item[H2 (Task Performance):] The Hybrid condition yields comparable
or higher mission accuracy, with a modest increase in completion time
attributable to device switching.

\item[H3 (Cross-Verification Behaviour):] In the Hybrid condition,
participants exhibit significantly more companion-display usage, with
CVI significantly greater than~1 during uncertainty triggers.
\end{description}

\subsection{Procedure}

The study followed a 50-minute protocol per participant:
(1)~consent and demographics (5\,min), (2)~Condition~1 with device
setup, route navigation with 5~missions, and in-app NASA-TLX/trust
survey (15\,min), (3)~return to start and Condition~2 with the
alternate mission set and in-app surveys (15\,min),
(4)~comparative preference survey on the companion display (10\,min),
and (5)~debriefing (5\,min). An experimenter followed 3\,m behind,
observing but not intervening unless a safety risk arose.

\subsection{Data Collection}

All interaction events were logged to a 15-column CSV at millisecond
resolution, capturing 35+ event types including
\texttt{WAYPOINT\_REACHED}, \texttt{BEAM\_SCREEN\_ON/OFF},
\texttt{TRIGGER\_ACTIVATED}, \texttt{CONFIDENCE\_RATED}, and
\texttt{VERIFICATION\_ANSWERED}. Head rotation was sampled
continuously. Glass-view video was captured for post-hoc analysis.


%% ----- 5. RESULTS -----

\section{Results}

\subsection{Overview and Data Quality}

All 24 participants completed both conditions, yielding 48 sessions
and 240 individual missions. No sessions were excluded. We conducted
mixed ANOVAs (condition $\times$ counterbalancing group) for all
primary DVs to check for order effects. No significant main effects of
order group or condition--order interactions were found (all
$p > .05$, all $\eta^2_p < .20$), confirming that counterbalancing
was effective.

Normality was assessed via Shapiro--Wilk tests. Where normality was
violated ($p < .05$), we used Wilcoxon signed-rank tests; otherwise,
paired $t$-tests. Effect sizes are reported as Cohen's $d$ for all
comparisons.

\subsection{Mission Accuracy (H2)}

Table~\ref{tab:accuracy} presents mission accuracy by type. Overall
accuracy did not differ significantly between conditions (Glass~Only
$75.0 \pm 17.0$\% vs.\ Hybrid $76.0 \pm 20.0$\%;
$W = 75.0$, $p = .942$, $d = -0.042$). However, accuracy varied
substantially by mission type. \textbf{Type~B (Ambiguous Decision)}
missions---those directly associated with uncertainty
triggers---showed the largest improvement, with Hybrid participants
achieving 72.9\% accuracy compared to 47.9\% in Glass~Only, a gain
of \textbf{+25.0 percentage points}. Type~A (Direction Verify) and
Type~C (Info Integration) missions showed smaller, non-significant
improvements.

The Type~B result is noteworthy: these missions required navigational
decisions during active uncertainty triggers, precisely the context
where the companion display's contextual information (map orientation,
POI details) should provide the greatest benefit. Type~A missions,
requiring simple attribute verification at the destination, showed a
ceiling effect in both conditions. Type~C missions, requiring
cross-location comparison, benefited modestly from the companion
display's comparison matrices.

\subsection{Completion Time (H2)}

Mean session completion time was $587.1 \pm 40.3$\,s for Glass~Only
and $615.4 \pm 60.6$\,s for Hybrid ($t(23) = -1.921$, $p = .067$,
$d = -0.392$). The difference was not statistically significant,
though the trend suggests a modest time overhead in the Hybrid
condition. Notably, mission-level analysis showed Hybrid participants
were slightly faster on individual missions across all types
(Fig.~\ref{fig:main_results}), suggesting that the session-level time
difference reflects device-switching overhead between missions rather
than slower navigation within missions.

\subsection{Trust and Confidence (H1)}

System trust and per-mission confidence both showed large, significant
improvements in the Hybrid condition.

\textbf{Trust.} The composite trust score (7~items, 7-point scale) was
significantly higher in Hybrid ($M = 4.93, SD = 0.38$) than
Glass~Only ($M = 4.34, SD = 0.37$; $t(23) = -5.02$, $p < .001$,
$d = -1.025$). Table~\ref{tab:trust} presents item-level results from
the in-app trust survey. Three items reached individual significance:
\emph{Reliability} ($p = .021$, $d = -0.525$), \emph{Confidence}
($p = .001$, $d = -0.934$), and \emph{Safety} ($p = .026$,
$d = -0.498$). The largest single-item effect was for the ``I feel
confident in the system's guidance'' item, with a near-unit effect
size---indicating that Hybrid participants felt substantially more
assured about the system's navigational guidance.

\textbf{Confidence.} Per-mission confidence ratings (collected after
each mission) showed an even larger effect: Glass~Only
$M = 3.60, SD = 0.46$ versus Hybrid $M = 4.69, SD = 0.32$
($t(23) = -11.43$, $p < .001$, $d = -2.333$). This very large effect
suggests that the availability of a verification channel fundamentally
changed how confident participants felt about their navigational
decisions, regardless of whether they actually consulted it.

\subsection{Cognitive Workload}

NASA-TLX total workload was significantly lower in the Hybrid
condition: Glass~Only $M = 3.84, SD = 0.47$ versus Hybrid
$M = 3.27, SD = 0.46$ ($t(23) = 4.42$, $p < .001$, $d = 0.902$).
This large effect indicates that the companion display reduced
perceived cognitive demand rather than adding to it---counter to the
concern that managing two devices increases workload.

Table~\ref{tab:tlx} presents subscale results. \emph{Effort} showed
the largest individual effect ($d = 0.631$, $p = .008$), suggesting
that Glass~Only participants perceived their navigational task as
requiring more sustained cognitive exertion. \emph{Temporal Demand}
approached significance ($p = .060$), consistent with Glass~Only
participants feeling more time pressure when navigating without a
verification channel. The in-app survey additionally revealed
significant effects for \emph{Mental Demand} ($p = .029$) and
\emph{Frustration} ($p = .009$), reinforcing that the Hybrid
condition was perceived as less cognitively taxing and less
frustrating.

\subsection{Cross-Verification Behaviour (H3)}

In the Hybrid condition, participants switched to the companion
display $M = 2.38$ times per session ($SD = 1.61$; $W < .001$,
$d = -1.475$), spending a total of $M = 24.1$\,s ($SD = 15.4$;
$p < .001$, $d = -1.564$) on the companion display per session. By
definition, Glass~Only participants had zero device switches.

However, the Cross-Verification Index was $\text{CVI} = 0.0$ for all
participants with switching data. No device switches occurred during
active trigger zones; \emph{all} companion-display consultations
were during non-trigger segments of the route. This pattern indicates
that participants did not reactively switch to the phone in response
to uncertainty triggers. Instead, they adopted a \textbf{proactive,
self-directed} information-seeking strategy, consulting the companion
display at self-chosen moments throughout the session.

\subsection{Effect Size Summary}

Figure~\ref{fig:trust_forest} summarises Cohen's $d$ effect sizes
across all primary and secondary DVs. The largest effects were
observed for per-mission confidence ($d = -2.333$), device switching
behaviour ($d = -1.475$ and $-1.564$), and trust ($d = -1.025$)---all
exceeding Cohen's threshold for large effects ($|d| > 0.8$). Cognitive
workload reduction ($d = 0.902$) was also a large effect. Task
performance measures (accuracy, completion time) showed small to
negligible effects ($|d| < 0.4$), suggesting that the Hybrid
condition's primary benefits are attitudinal and experiential rather
than strictly performance-based.

%% ----- 6. DISCUSSION -----

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Trust Calibration and the Cross-Verification Loop}

Our results provide strong support for H1: the Hybrid condition
produced significantly higher trust ($d = -1.025$) and per-mission
confidence ($d = -2.333$). These large effects suggest that the mere
\emph{availability} of a companion display fundamentally changes how
users assess system reliability. This aligns with Chiou and Lee's
updated trust framework~\cite{Chiou:2023:TAD}, which emphasises that
providing a ``responsive alternative information source'' supports the
\emph{process of trusting}---not just the endpoint of calibrated
trust. In our system, the Beam Pro served precisely this role: a
readily accessible verification channel that participants could
consult when they chose, enabling what we term a
\emph{cross-verification loop} between the ambient guidance on the
glasses and the detailed information on the phone.

The distributed cognition perspective~\cite{Hutchins:1995:CIW,
Hollan:2000:DCT} further explains these results. By distributing the
``where'' and ``what'' functions across two devices with distinct
interaction modalities, the system created complementary
representations that supported more robust navigational
decision-making. Wickens' multiple resource
theory~\cite{Wickens:2002:MRP} predicts that spatial (glasses) and
verbal/detailed (phone) information channels should interfere
minimally, which the reduced TLX scores ($d = 0.902$) support.

\subsection{Performance Trade-offs}

H2 received partial support. Overall accuracy was statistically
equivalent between conditions, but the composition of this null effect
is informative. Type~B missions---those embedded within uncertainty
triggers---showed a 25 percentage-point accuracy improvement in the
Hybrid condition, while Type~A (ceiling effect) and Type~C (modest
gain) showed smaller differences. This pattern suggests that the
companion display's greatest performance contribution occurs precisely
when guidance is degraded or ambiguous, consistent with the design
intention.

Completion time showed a marginal increase ($p = .067$, $d = -0.392$)
at the session level. The within-mission analysis revealed faster
individual mission times in the Hybrid condition across all types,
suggesting that the session-level overhead reflects transition time
(device handling, re-orientation) rather than slower navigation per se.
The net cost of approximately 28\,s per session (4.8\% overhead) is
modest relative to the substantial gains in trust and workload
reduction.

\subsection{Voluntary Cross-Verification}

H3 received partial support in an unexpected direction. While
companion-display usage was significantly elevated in the Hybrid
condition (as predicted), the CVI of zero indicates that this usage
was entirely \emph{voluntary and proactive} rather than
\emph{trigger-reactive}. Participants did not wait for uncertainty to
arise before consulting the phone; they integrated it into their
navigation workflow at self-chosen moments.

This finding resonates with Pirolli and Card's information foraging
theory~\cite{Pirolli:1999:IF}: participants may have developed an
internal ``information scent'' model in which periodic companion-display
checks maintained a sense of navigational certainty, pre-empting the
need for reactive checks when triggers occurred. Alternatively,
Parasuraman and Manzey's~\cite{Parasuraman:2010:CAB} automation
complacency framework suggests that Glass~Only participants may have
relied on the AR arrows without actively questioning their accuracy,
while Hybrid participants' awareness of an alternative source
promoted a more vigilant navigational stance.

\subsection{Design Implications}

Our findings suggest several principles for hybrid AR navigation:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Availability over enforcement.} The companion display's
benefits arose from its \emph{optional availability}, not from forced
usage. Adaptive systems should maintain user agency rather than
prescribing when to consult secondary devices.

\item \textbf{WHERE/WHAT separation.} The functional division between
ambient directional guidance (glasses) and detailed contextual
information (phone) leverages the strengths of each device while
minimising the cognitive cost of switching.

\item \textbf{Value per switch.} Following Rashid et
al.~\cite{Rashid:2012:CDS}, hybrid configurations succeed when each
display switch provides sufficient information value to offset
attentional costs. Mission-specific contextual content (maps,
comparison matrices, hints) provides this value.
\end{enumerate}


%% ----- 7. LIMITATIONS -----

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

The study uses a single indoor environment (university building
basement), limiting generalisability to other building types and
scales. The within-subjects design with two mission sets, while
controlling individual differences, introduces potential learning
effects that counterbalancing mitigates but cannot fully eliminate.
The four uncertainty triggers are designer-defined and may not capture
all naturalistic sources of navigational uncertainty. The CVI~$= 0$
finding, while revealing, limits our ability to model the relationship
between specific trigger types and cross-verification behaviour;
future designs could include longer trigger durations or more salient
uncertainty manipulations.

Future work will extend the paradigm to multi-floor environments with
vertical transitions, incorporate eye-tracking to directly measure
gaze transitions between devices, explore adaptive trigger generation
based on real-time tracking quality, and conduct longitudinal studies
of hybrid device adoption to assess whether the trust and workload
benefits persist with experience.


%% ----- 8. CONCLUSION -----

\section{Conclusion}

We presented a hybrid smart glasses--smartphone indoor navigation
system and evaluated it in a within-subjects experiment ($N{=}24$)
comparing Glass~Only and Hybrid conditions under navigational
uncertainty. Three key findings emerged: (1)~the Hybrid condition
significantly improved system trust ($d = -1.025$) and per-mission
confidence ($d = -2.333$) while reducing cognitive workload
($d = 0.902$); (2)~ambiguous-decision missions showed a 25
percentage-point accuracy gain, demonstrating that the companion
display's greatest contribution occurs during degraded guidance; and
(3)~participants adopted a voluntary, proactive information-seeking
strategy (CVI${=}0$) rather than reactively consulting the companion
display during triggers. These results support the value of
distributing navigational information across complementary
devices---ambient directional cues on AR glasses and detailed
contextual information on a smartphone---as a design strategy for
trustworthy indoor wayfinding under uncertainty.
